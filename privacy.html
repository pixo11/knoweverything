A BRIEF SUMMARY FOR PEOPLE WHO SKIP TO THE END: We collect your data. We use it. We store it. We think about it. We occasionally share it with partners, which we will describe in careful language designed to sound less alarming than it is. Gary has access to the database. We are still dealing with that. The rest of this document explains all of the above in more words.

SECTION 1What We Know About You

1.1 — What You Told Us

You filled out 100 questions. We have all of it. Your name, your address, your zodiac sign, your dominant hand, your relationship status, your diet, your sleep schedule, your opinion on toilet paper, your spirit animal, your post-apocalypse survival strategy, your genie wish, your tombstone text, and the number of browser tabs you had open. All of it is in a spreadsheet. The spreadsheet is colour coded. See Terms of Service Article 11.2 regarding the colour system. We are still not telling you.

1.2 — What You Didn't Tell Us But We Inferred

Our algorithm has generated the following inferences from your answers, which we hold as data in their own right:

  • Your approximate stress level at the time of completion (detectable from answer speed on the open text questions)
  • Whether you paused before answering the zodiac question (most people who selected "I don't believe in this" paused; we have thoughts about this)
  • Whether your choice of spirit animal matches your self-image or your aspirational self (these diverge more than people expect)
  • Your approximate 11pm refrigerator frequency (see Terms of Service Section 5.3)
  • Whether you answered the "how dramatic are you" question dramatically (a meta-result; we treasure these)

1.3 — What Gary Knows

Gary has access to the same database as the rest of the team. Gary cannot read. Gary can, however, sit on the keyboard at statistically impactful moments. On March 14th, 2023, Gary's keyboard intervention resulted in the accidental deletion of 47 user records and the creation of one record that belonged to no one and had answered every question with "7." That record still exists. We cannot delete it. We have grown attached to it. Its spirit animal is listed as "a Roomba." We think that's fitting.

SECTION 3The Data We Know You Know We Have

3.1 — Obvious Data

Your name. Your address. Your preferences. You gave us these. You know we have them. There is no dramatic reveal here. This is the boring section of the Privacy Policy and we respect you too much to stretch it out.

3.2 — Less Obvious Data

Your implicit personality score, derived from the combination of all 100 answers viewed as a single dataset. The score is not a number. It is a word. We selected the word from a list of 200 options. We are not telling you your word, but we will say this: the word is accurate, you have heard it applied to you before, probably by someone who knew you well, and your reaction to reading that last sentence is also data.

SECTION 4Who Else Sees Your Data

4.1 — Internal Access

The full team (4 humans, 1 bot named Gerald who only sends thumbs-ups, and Gary). Gary should not have access. We have changed the password twice. Gary was present both times and appears to have memorised it. We are exploring other solutions including a keyboard tray with a lock on it, a decoy spreadsheet, and simply accepting that Gary is a part of this organisation now in a way that exceeds his original mandate as "office cat."

4.2 — Third Parties

We share data with the following categories of third parties:

Service providers: Companies that help us run the platform, store data, and send the Tuesday newsletter. They see your data for technical reasons. They are not supposed to have opinions about it. Some of them probably do.

Analytics partners: Entities that help us understand how people use KnowEverything. They receive anonymised data, which means they know what "most users" did but not specifically you. Except in cases where "most users" did something unusual, in which case it is probably quite clear who it was. We are looking at you, the person who selected both "avocado (technically a fruit, overpriced)" and "I work for exposure." You know who you are.

Research institutions: We have shared aggregated data with two academic institutions studying the psychology of self-disclosure in online contexts. Neither institution was prepared for the volume of people who selected "raccoon" as their spirit animal. One researcher described the raccoon data as "the most unexpectedly coherent subgroup I have encountered in 15 years of work." We feel seen by this. The raccoons feel seen by this. They know who they are.

"Partners": The Terms of Service refers to "partners" in Article 12.1(b). These are advertisers. We are telling you now. We thought it was better to be direct. The Privacy Policy is where we keep our honesty. The Terms of Service is where we keep the Penguin Clause.

4.3 — Entities We Will Not Share Your Data With

We will not share your data with: your parents (unless you ask us to, which you can do via the Tuesday newsletter's optional "Share with family" module, which nobody has used and which we keep available on principle); your ex (this was considered and rejected in a process we are proud of); anyone who would use your tombstone text against you; or the government, except as required by applicable law, which is a clause every Privacy Policy has to include and which we include here acknowledging that it sounds sinister but is genuinely just a legal formality. Probably.

SECTION 5Cookies

5.1 — The Cookie Situation

We use cookies. Cookies are small files that websites store on your device to remember things about you. If you have been on the internet in the last ten years, you have agreed to roughly 4,000 cookie policies and you are not reading this one more carefully than you read the others, which is fine, which is our point.

5.2 — Types of Cookies We Use

Essential cookies: Required for the form to function. You cannot opt out of these. If you tried, the form would stop working and we would both be frustrated.

Analytics cookies: Tell us how people interact with the form. For example: 73% of users pause on the toilet paper question for longer than any other question. 12% change their answer. 3% change it back. We find this deeply human. Analytics cookies help us find this kind of thing. We would not give them up.

Preference cookies: Remember your settings if you return. You probably won't return. Most people fill the form out once and then tell their friends about it. That is the intended experience. If you do return, however, we would like to remember you. It seems only fair.

Gary cookies: Appear occasionally when Gary walks across the keyboard during a deployment. Their behaviour is unpredictable. They do not collect data, as far as we can determine. They do appear to affect the colour scheme slightly in ways we cannot reproduce. We have left them in because removing them caused worse problems.

SECTION 6Your Rights

6.1 — The Right to Access

You may request a copy of your data at any time. We will send it within 30 business days, in a format that is technically machine-readable and also quite interesting to look at. Several people have requested their data and reported that it was "weirder than expected" and "more accurate than was comfortable." We consider both outcomes successful.

6.2 — The Right to Correction

If any data we hold about you is inaccurate, you may request a correction. This applies to factual information (your address, your name). It does not apply to inferred data (your personality word, your Soul Value™, your energy profile) because those are not incorrect — they are computed. If you disagree with a computed result, you may submit a written objection. The objection will be read, considered, and then weighed against the algorithm. The algorithm currently has a compelling track record. We will be honest about this.

6.3 — The Right to Deletion

You may request deletion of your data. We will delete it. The process takes approximately 14 days and involves six systems, one of which is a spreadsheet that Gary may have locally cached somewhere and we genuinely do not know how to check. We will make a sincere and well-documented effort. The record of our effort to delete your data is itself stored as data. We understand the irony. The Terms of Service mentions this in the Miscellaneous section, buried in the section that is the word "Miscellaneous" in different fonts. It is in the bold one, for what it's worth.

6.4 — The Right to Not Have Your 10-Year Message Sent

You may opt out of receiving your message to yourself in 10 years. We ask you to reconsider, because in our experience the people who most want to opt out are the ones who most need to receive it. But we will respect the choice. The opt-out form is in the Tuesday newsletter, under a section called "Things We'd Rather You Didn't Do But We Respect Your Autonomy."

SECTION 7Data Security

7.1 — How We Protect Your Data

We use industry-standard security practices, which is a phrase that sounds reassuring and mostly means: our passwords are strong (except for the one Gary knows), our servers are encrypted, our team has completed data security training (except Gary, who was present for it but is a cat), and we conduct regular security audits that have not yet uncovered any issues beyond the Gary situation, which is ongoing.

7.2 — The Gary Situation

Gary is a cat who has, through a series of keyboard-based interventions and what security professionals describe as "statistically improbable coincidences," acquired access to portions of our database that exceed his intended role as ambient morale support. We have conducted three internal reviews of the Gary situation. Each review concluded that Gary has not misused his access, largely because Gary is a cat and does not have goals in the traditional sense. We acknowledge this is not a satisfying security finding. We are continuing to monitor.

SECTION 8Children's Privacy

KnowEverything is intended for users aged 13 and above. If you are under 13 and have filled out this form anyway: we are genuinely impressed by your stamina. 100 questions is a lot. Also, please tell a parent or guardian you did this. Not because we will do anything with your data that requires their consent, but because the conversation will be interesting and we believe in interesting conversations.

SECTION 9Changes to This Policy

We may update this Privacy Policy at any time. Changes will be posted here and announced in the Tuesday newsletter, under a section called "We Changed Some Things." We will be transparent about what changed and why. We believe transparency is important. We also believe most people will not read the update. These two beliefs coexist without contradiction. That is also, in some small way, a finding about human behaviour. Everything here is a finding about human behaviour. That is the point of all of this.
